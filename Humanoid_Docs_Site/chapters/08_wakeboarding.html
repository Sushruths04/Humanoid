CHAPTER 08

The Wakeboarding Experiment

Our flagship new task: teach a G1 the hardest moment in wakeboarding — the deep-water start — in a dry, sand-like simulation.

The idea

When a beginner wakeboards, the boat yanks them from a floating crouch up onto the board at ~30 km/h. Almost everyone fails the start — they stand up too fast, pull the rope, or let the board dig in, and face-plant. We model this on land: the G1 sits crouched, feet on a board, holding a rope handle; a rope force ramps to 30 km/h; it must learn the correct body angles to rise to a stable ride.

Why "sand instead of water"?

Simulating water is expensive and unstable. Instead we use a board that slides on a low-friction ground — a dry analog that keeps the core physics (a tow force dragging a board + rider) while staying fast and reliable. We're upfront that this is a deliberate abstraction.

How it's built

🛹

Board + rope

A board welded to the feet; the rope is a virtual anchor moving at 30 km/h that pulls the handle via a capped spring force.

🎯

Reward = the 5 rules

Board angle, straight arms, handle at hips, gradual rise, don't pull the rope. (Chapter 4.)

📈

Curriculum

Pull speed ramps 10→30 km/h only as the robot succeeds — never start at the hardest setting.

🎭

AMP style

A "looks-like-a-real-start" reward so the motion is natural, not robotic.

All the framework-independent logic is written and tested; the Isaac-Lab glue is written with a Day-1 GPU checklist. First GPU session is a ~2–4 h checklist, not blind debugging.

The bigger vision — a coaching robot

Once the robot knows the ideal start angles, it becomes a coach: a webcam reads a human learner's knee/hip/arm angles and compares them to the robot's optimal pose, giving feedback ("bend your knees more", "straighten your arms"). The whole environment is angle-centric specifically to make this possible later.

Interview answer — "tell me about the wakeboarding project."
"It frames a wakeboard deep-water start as a getting-up-under-external-pull task. The G1 starts crouched with feet welded to a board and a rope modeled as a velocity-target spring ramping to 30 km/h. The reward encodes wakeboard biomechanics — positive board angle, straight arms, gradual leg extension, no pulling the rope — plus a pull-speed curriculum and AMP style, trained with PPO in two stages à la HumanUP. It reuses the same Isaac Lab + G1 + RSL-RL stack as my locomotion work."
