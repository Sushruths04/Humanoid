CHAPTER 03

How a Humanoid Sits & Stands

Getting up and staying up sounds trivial — for a robot it's one of the hardest motions. Here's how it learns.

Why standing up is hard

  • It's not repetitive. Walking is a rhythm you repeat. Getting up is a one-time sequence — the robot must discover which body parts to push with, and when.
  • Contacts keep changing. Hands, knees, feet touch the ground at different moments. The robot has to invent that contact schedule itself.
  • The reward is sparse. "You're standing!" only happens at the very end. We have to break it into smaller hints (get taller → get upright → stay balanced).

The two-stage recipe (HumanUP)

We follow a published method called HumanUP that taught a real G1 to stand from lying down (78% success). It splits learning into two stages — the same idea works for the wakeboard rise:

Stage 1 gives the robot freedom to flail and discover a working trajectory. Stage 2 takes that discovered motion, slows it ~8×, and re-trains it to be smooth, energy-efficient, and robust to bumps — the version you'd put on real hardware.

How "balance" is measured

Two simple signals tell us if the robot is doing well:

📐

Uprightness

How vertical the torso is — measured by projecting gravity onto the body's up-axis. 1.0 = perfectly upright, 0 = lying down.

⏱️

Episode length

Episodes end the instant the robot falls. So a long episode means it stayed up. Our robust policy reaches 981 / 1000 — it almost never falls.

Connecting the dots → The wakeboard start (Chapter 8) is literally a "get up" motion, but with a rope yanking you forward at 30 km/h. So we reuse this exact two-stage recipe plus the uprightness/balance signals.
Interview answer — "how does the robot learn to stand?"
"Getting up is non-periodic with shifting contacts and sparse reward, so I use a two-stage approach à la HumanUP: a discovery stage with weak regularization to find a feasible trajectory, then a deployable stage that tracks a slowed version under strict smoothness and torque penalties plus domain randomization. Balance is shaped via a torso-uprightness term (projected gravity) and rewarded implicitly through episode length, since a fall terminates the episode."
