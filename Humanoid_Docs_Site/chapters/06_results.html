Evaluation & Results

What we measured, the real numbers, and how we keep ourselves honest about what's proven vs. just "it ran."

The honesty rule

A number only counts if there's a file backing it. Every result below has a real artifact on disk (a training log or an eval summary). "The pipeline ran" is not the same as "the task is benchmarked" — and we label which is which.

What "981 / 1000" actually means

Episodes are 1000 steps long and end early if the robot falls. So 981/1000 means that, even on rough terrain with randomized joints, the robot stays on its feet for ~98% of the episode. It's our single most defensible result — fully backed by a training log.

How we evaluate (the metrics)

Success rate

Did it complete the task (e.g. reach a stable wakeboard ride within 6 s)?

Fall rate

How often it ends in a fall.

Time-to-stand

How quickly it gets up.

Form adherence

% of time the board angle / arm straightness stay in the correct band.

Smoothness & energy

Is the motion jittery? How much effort?

Separation score

(Language) do different commands actually produce different behavior?

Comparisons we produce (the interview slides)

  • Across difficulty — success vs pull speed (20 / 25 / 30 / 35 km/h).
  • Ablations — remove one ingredient (curriculum, biomechanics rewards, AMP, randomization) and show exactly which metric it breaks.
  • Stage 1 vs Stage 2 — same success, but far smoother & more natural after stage 2.

Interview answer — "how did you evaluate it?"
"Task-level metrics: success rate, fall rate, time-to-stand, form-adherence, smoothness, and energy — each written to a JSON from a held-out rollout. I run difficulty sweeps and ablations so I can attribute behavior to specific reward/curriculum components. My strongest backed result is robust locomotion: mean reward 22.8 with 981/1000 episode length under rough terrain and ±25% actuator randomization, straight from the training log."
