CHAPTER 01

The Big Picture

Before any code: what are we actually building, and why a humanoid at all?

In one sentence

We are teaching a humanoid robot — the Unitree G1 — to control its own body (stand, balance, walk, follow commands, and even start on a wakeboard) by letting it practice millions of times inside a physics simulator and rewarding it when it does the right thing. That trial-and-error-with-rewards method is Reinforcement Learning (RL).

The video-game analogy. Imagine a video-game character that learns to walk by trying random movements, getting points when it moves correctly and losing points when it falls. Do that a few hundred million times in fast-forward, across thousands of copies at once, and it figures out how to walk. Our "game" is just a physically accurate one, so the skill transfers to a real robot.

Why a humanoid (and why the G1)?

  • The world is built for humans. Stairs, doorways, tools — a human-shaped robot can use them without redesigning the environment.
  • It's the hardest, most general body. Two legs, many joints, easy to fall over. If you can control a humanoid, you've solved a very general control problem.
  • The G1 is real, affordable, and supported. Unitree's G1 has a ready-made simulation model in our tools, so what we train in sim can run on the real robot.

The two projects in this documentation

Both run on the same machine/stack — they're two different tasks, not two different systems. That's why they share a Docker image and the same training tools.

The core loop you'll see everywhere

The robot observes its body state, the policy (its "brain", a neural network) picks an action (joint movements), the simulator gives a reward for how good that was, and the algorithm (PPO) nudges the policy to get more reward next time. Repeat ~billions of times.

Interview answer — "what is this project?"
"It's reinforcement learning for whole-body humanoid control on the Unitree G1, trained in NVIDIA Isaac Lab. The policy observes proprioception and task info, outputs joint targets, and is optimized with PPO against a reward that encodes the task plus correct biomechanics. I have a locomotion track (walking, robustness, language/vision) and a separate wakeboard-start task, both on the same Isaac Lab + RSL-RL + GPU stack."
